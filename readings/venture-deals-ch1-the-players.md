# Venture Deals — Chapter 1: The Players

> Source: Venture Deals (Feld & Mendelson), Chapter 1, pp. 3–16

---

## The Entrepreneur

- The center of the entrepreneurial universe — founders can't outsource their involvement in a financing to lawyers
- Key issues in a financing can only be resolved by the entrepreneur
- Cofounders: relationship often frays over time (stress, competence, personality). Investors try to anticipate this via vesting, drag-along rights, co-sale rights

---

## The Venture Capitalist (VC)

### Hierarchy inside a VC firm

| Title | Role |
|---|---|
| **Managing Director (MD) / General Partner (GP)** | Final investment decision-makers. Sit on boards. These are the people who matter. |
| **Partner** | Title-inflated in many firms — may or may not be a decision-maker. Always confirm if they're an MD/GP. |
| **Principal / Director** | Junior deal professionals. Some deal responsibility but need MD support to close. Cannot make final decisions. |
| **Associate** | Not deal partners. Support MDs: source deals, due diligence, write memos, manage cap tables. Usually 2-year programs. |
| **Analyst** | Most junior. Numbers and memos. Limited power. |
| **Venture Partner / Operating Partner** | Part-time, experienced entrepreneurs. Can advocate for deals but need MD support. |
| **Entrepreneur in Residence (EIR)** | Experienced entrepreneurs parked at the firm while figuring out their next company. Help with intros, diligence, networking. |

**Key rule:** Always develop a direct relationship with an MD or GP. Anyone else is unlikely to be at the firm long-term.

## Financing Round Nomenclature

Stages signal how early/late a company is — used to target the right type of investor.

| Stage | Typical Use |
|---|---|
| **Pre-Seed** | Before seed — very early, often friends & family |
| **Seed / Series Seed** | First institutional money |
| **Series A** | Early stage — formerly "Series B" before inflation |
| **Series B, C, D** | Mid stage — growth capital |
| **Series E+** | Late stage — often pre-IPO |
| **Series B-1, B-2** | Extension rounds — same terms, different price from preceding round |

> "No magic or legal definition in naming rounds." Labels just signal stage.

## Types of VC Firms

| Type | Fund Size | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Micro VC** | <$15M | Seed/early. Often ex-angel investors. |
| **Seed Stage** | Up to $150M | First institutional money. Rarely invest past Series A. |
| **Early Stage** | $100M–$300M | Seed + Series A, occasionally Series B. |
| **Mid Stage / Growth** | $200M–$1B | Series B and later. Company is "clearly working." |
| **Late Stage** | $1B+ | Pre-IPO. Includes hedge funds, crossover investors, sovereign wealth funds. |

**Key rule:** Match the investor to your stage. Targeting a firm that doesn't invest in your stage is wasted energy.

## Angel Investors

- Individual investors — often active in seed/early rounds
- Can be professional investors, successful entrepreneurs, friends, family
- **Super angels:** Very active angels who make many small investments. Often experienced entrepreneurs with prior exits.
- **Super angel → Micro VC:** When super angels raise a fund from others, they have the same fiduciary responsibilities as a VC.
- VCs may use pay-to-play and drag-along rights to force angel behavior in difficult follow-on rounds

**Watch out:** Don't let angels hold you hostage. For a diffuse group of friends/family investors, consider a special-purpose limited partnership controlled by one of them.

---

## The Syndicate

- A collection of investors in a financing round (VCs, angels, strategics, corporations, law firms)
- Usually has a **lead investor** who negotiates terms for the whole syndicate
- **Party round:** Many investors making small investments — looks great in a press release but you get little attention from any of them. Worst of both worlds at next round.
- **Key rule:** Get investors to agree the lead investor can speak for the syndicate on terms. Don't negotiate the same deal multiple times.

---

## The Lawyer

- Only two things that really matter in a financing: **economics and control**
- A good lawyer keeps you focused on what matters; a bad one fights the wrong battles and runs up bills on both sides
- Your lawyer is a reflection on you — bad behavior carries into your post-deal partnership with investors
- **Fees (as of 2019):** Early stage financing: $5k–$20k. Typical financing: $20k–$40k. Insist on a fee cap.

---

## The Accountant

- Rarely involved in early stage (except cross-border or LLC → C Corp restructuring)
- More important in later rounds: stock option pricing, tax filings, tax credits

---

## The Banker / Investment Banker

- Rarely seen in early stage. Common in later rounds ($50M+) targeting strategic/nontraditional investors
- If an early stage company has hired a banker: red flag — either they couldn't raise on their own, or bad advice

---

## The Mentor

- Different from advisers (who often charge fees)
- Mentors help because someone once helped them. Often become early angel investors or take small equity grants.
- No fee arrangement up front — and that's the right way
