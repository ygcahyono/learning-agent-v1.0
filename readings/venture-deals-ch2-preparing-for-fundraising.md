## Choosing the Right Lawyer

- Startup's legal adviser guides the company from incorporation → acquisition/IPO
- Key criteria: experience with startups, personal connection, communication style
- Don't obsess on hourly rate — a competent startup lawyer is worth far more than a cheaper generalist
- The lawyer is a representative of your company — their reputation becomes yours

## Proactive vs. Reactive

Proactive preparation before the first term sheet is critical. Key steps:

### 1. Corporate Structure
- **Must be a C Corporation incorporated in Delaware**
- VCs cannot invest in LLCs or S Corps (pass-through tax entities)
- Delaware is universally accepted; some accept CA or NY but Delaware is the safe default
- Qualified to do business in every state where you operate

### 2. Due Diligence Readiness — The Data Site

> "The due diligence process is a key component of any venture financing."

Set up a **data site** before you go out to raise. Can be Dropbox, Box, Google Drive, or Carta. Populate it with:

**Legal documents:**
- Certificate of Incorporation
- Bylaws
- Board and shareholder minutes and actions
- Capital stock documentation (list of all holders and rights)
- Agreements granting preemptive or preferential rights

**Financial documents:**
- Financial records and budgets
- Major customer lists
- Employment agreements

**Why this matters for BI:** The BI team is often responsible for the financial dashboards and metrics that go into investor-facing materials. Clean, organized data is the foundation of a credible data room.

### 3. Cap Table

> "A cap table is a complete list of all of a company's securities and their ownership organized in a spreadsheet."

- Shows who owns what percentage of the company and on what terms
- Associates at VC firms are often the ones who spend the most time with the cap table
- You must be well-versed in the cap table before investor conversations — investors will ask

**What the cap table contains:**
- All securities (common stock, preferred stock, options, warrants, convertible notes)
- Who holds each security
- How much they paid
- What rights they have

## Intellectual Property (IP)

For many investors, IP is what makes a company a compelling investment.

IP = patents + copyrights (code) + trademarks (brand) + trade secrets (almost anything)

**What to prepare:**
- Clear, documented chain of title for all IP
- Confidentiality + IP assignment agreements signed by:
  - All employees (Proprietary Inventions and Assignment Agreement — include with every offer)
  - All contractors (IP provisions in independent contractor agreement)
  - All advisers (even if you trust them)
  - Any cofounder who has since left

**Key risk:** Founders who started working on the company while still employed elsewhere — the prior employer's PIIA may claim ownership of IP created on "company time" or in related domains.

**Red flag:** Missing a single IP assignment agreement can kill a deal.

## Why This Chapter Matters for the BI Manager Role

During a fundraising process (especially due diligence), the BI team:
- Maintains the financial data that goes into the data room
- Builds or validates the metrics and KPIs investors will scrutinize
- Ensures the numbers in the cap table model reconcile with actuals
- Produces the clean financial history investors audit

The BI Manager who understands what's in a data room — and why each piece is there — can proactively ensure the company is always fundraising-ready.
