# Venture Deals — Chapter 3: How to Raise Money

> Source: Venture Deals (Feld & Mendelson), Chapter 3, pp. 24–40

---

## Mindset

- Once you flip to active fundraising, you must be **all in** — no half-measures
- Failure is part of entrepreneurship — attitude impacts outcome
- Don't send mass emails; build real relationships

---

## Determine How Much to Raise

**Don't target the wrong investors by raising the wrong amount.**

| Raise amount | Who to talk to |
|---|---|
| ~$500k seed | Angels, seed VCs, super angels, micro VCs, early stage investors |
| ~$10M+ | Larger VC firms — need a lead who can write a $5M+ check |

**How to think about the amount:**
1. Identify the **next meaningful milestone** (ship first product, reach X users, hit $Y revenue)
2. Calculate **monthly burn rate** to get there
3. Add a time cushion (typically 12 months)
4. Raise that specific number — no ranges

> "Don't believe in ranges. $5M to $7M — is it $5M or $7M?"

A range signals you haven't thought hard about how much you actually need. It also changes the math for what role a VC can play (lead vs. co-lead).

## Fundraising Materials

### 1. Short Description (Elevator Pitch)
- 1–3 paragraphs: product, team, business
- Goes in the body of an email — not an attachment
- Must stand alone without you talking through it

### 2. Executive Summary
- Up to 3 pages
- Contents: problem you're solving + why it matters, why your product is better, why your team is right, high-level financials
- Your first substantive impression — will be passed around the VC firm if there's interest
- Signals your communication and analytical skills

### 3. Presentation (Pitch Deck)
- 10–20 slides covering: problem, opportunity, team, competition/advantage, plan, current status, financials, use of proceeds, milestones
- **Less is more** — most good decks are 10 slides or fewer
- Form matters: well-designed slides get better responses

### 4. Business Plan
- 30+ pages — rarely read by VCs (especially early stage)
- Occasionally requested — be prepared to respond
- More useful as a discipline for founders thinking through the business than as a fundraising tool

### 5. Private Placement Memorandum (PPM)
- Business plan wrapped in legal disclaimers
- Used when investment bankers are involved and raising from large institutional investors
- Early stage companies using PPMs: red flag — waste of money and time

### 6. Detailed Financial Model
- 100% of financial predictions are wrong — but expenses reveal how you think about the business
- VCs focus on: (1) assumptions behind revenue forecast, (2) monthly burn rate
- In later rounds: historical financials, unit economics, cost structure, key metrics matter a lot more

### 7. Demo / Prototype
- More important than a business plan for early stage
- Shows vision + ability to execute
- VCs want to interact with it, not just observe

## Due Diligence Materials

After a term sheet is signed, expect requests for:
- Cap tables
- Material customer agreements
- Employment agreements
- Board meeting minutes

**Prepare these before going out to raise** — being slow on diligence requests stalls the deal.

**Golden rule:** Never hide anything. Full disclosure builds trust. Undisclosed issues will come out eventually and damage the relationship.

## Finding the Right VC

- Best source: other entrepreneurs who have worked with the VC
- Research: what stage do they invest in? what sectors? who are the decision-makers?
- Warm introductions beat cold emails every time
- Target the right stage — one of the most common mistakes is approaching firms that don't invest at your stage

---

## Finding a Lead VC

A lead VC issues the term sheet, drives the financing, and becomes your most active investor.

**Four types of VC responses:**

| Type | Behavior | How to Handle |
|---|---|---|
| **Clearly interested, wants to lead** | Actively engages, moves quickly | Prioritize — this is your target |
| **Passes** | Says no | Move on — don't waste time trying to change their mind |
| **"Maybe"** | Interested but not engaging | Keep warm — bring them in once a lead emerges |
| **"Slow no"** | Never says no, never moves forward | Treat as a no — stop spending time |

**Key rule:** Never tell one VC who else you're talking to — it creates coordination between them that doesn't help you.

**Timing:** Allow 3–6 months for a competitive process. Less = desperation. More = no urgency.

## How VCs Decide to Invest

Typical process:
1. Initial contact (warm intro is much better than cold)
2. VC begins informal due diligence: "I'm taking exploration to the next level"
3. Multiple meetings, presentations, data requests
4. **Monday partner meeting** — present to the full partnership
5. Term sheet issued (or process stalls)

**Informal diligence requests:** Presentations, projections, customer pipeline, competitive analysis, team bios

**Key insight:** Ensure a GP/MD is actively involved early — if you're only talking to an associate, you may be in a data collection exercise, not a real funding process.

## Closing the Deal

Two-step close:

### Step 1: Sign the Term Sheet
- Nonbinding but signals serious intent
- Most executed term sheets lead to a financing — reputable early stage VCs can't afford to walk away
- Later-stage firms often have an **investment committee** final approval step — term sheets are not guarantees

### Step 2: Sign Definitive Documents + Receive Cash
- Lawyers draft 100+ pages of documentation from the term sheet
- Company responds to formal due diligence requests
- Deal can blow up if: (a) unexpected bad facts surface, (b) lawyers behave poorly and create tension, (c) company does something stupid during drafting

**Key rule:** Manage the closing process daily. Don't let lawyer drama become VC drama — call the VC directly before escalating.

## What This Means for BI

In a fundraising process, BI's role is central to:

1. **Preparing the financial model** — burn rate, unit economics, revenue assumptions that go into fundraising materials
2. **Building the data room** — financial history, KPI dashboards, cohort analysis investors will scrutinize
3. **Supporting the diligence process** — responding fast and accurately to investor data requests
4. **Maintaining a single source of truth** — the metrics in the pitch deck must reconcile with actuals in the data room
5. **Speed** — stalling on diligence requests is a deal killer; clean data infrastructure makes the company fast

A BI Manager who has lived through a fundraising process knows: investors are pattern-matching against your metrics. If your numbers are inconsistent across documents, that's a red flag.
